# Project Story

## The Starting Point

The expert network industry is well-established — GLG alone has been operating since 1998 — but the market remains surprisingly opaque to outsiders. Provider websites describe their services in broad terms. Pricing is rarely published. Compliance practices are framed positively but seldom compared. AI capabilities are claimed with varying levels of specificity.

For someone evaluating expert network providers for the first time — a new associate at a PE firm, a consulting team expanding its research capabilities, a corporate strategy group exploring the category — the information landscape is disorienting.

## The Observation

There was no independent resource that organized this information. Vendor directories existed, but they were either pay-to-play, overly generic, or maintained by the providers themselves. Industry reports from Integrity Research, Third Bridge, and others offered valuable data but were typically gated and expensive.

The gap was a public, structured, independent guide — something that did for expert networks what comparison sites do for software categories, but with the editorial care and source transparency that a professional audience expects.

## What the Project Set Out to Build

Not a startup. Not a marketplace. Not a lead-generation funnel. Just a well-researched, well-structured resource that helps serious buyers make better-informed decisions about expert network providers.

The goals were specific:

1. **Map the market.** Identify and profile the major providers, including pure-play networks, hybrid platforms, and technology-first entrants.
2. **Make it comparable.** Structure provider data so that buyers can filter, sort, and compare across meaningful dimensions — not just read marketing summaries.
3. **Be transparent about certainty.** Not all information is equally reliable. Build a system that labels the confidence level of each claim.
4. **Cover the buyer journey.** Support users at different stages: category education, initial discovery, shortlisting, and side-by-side evaluation.
5. **Stay current.** Track industry developments — acquisitions, partnerships, product launches, regulatory changes — so the resource stays relevant.

## How the Scope Evolved

The project started as a simple provider directory: name, description, a few data points. As the research progressed, the scope expanded naturally:

- Providers had enough variation in pricing models to justify a dedicated pricing guide.
- Compliance postures were complex enough to deserve structured treatment rather than a single badge.
- AI capabilities — matching, synthesis, Q&A — were evolving rapidly and needed their own tracking.
- The compare tool emerged because a directory alone does not support side-by-side evaluation.
- Buyer guides by industry (consulting, PE, hedge funds) became necessary because different buyer types weight different criteria.
- The news feed filled a gap: there was no single place tracking expert network industry developments in a curated, categorized format.

Each addition was driven by a specific user question that the existing pages could not answer.

## Reflection

The hardest part of this project is not the technology. It is the editorial judgment: knowing what to include, what to leave out, when to state a fact, and when to flag uncertainty. The expert network industry does not publish standardized data. Provider claims vary in precision. Third-party sources sometimes conflict.

Building a resource that is useful without being misleading requires constant attention to framing, language, and evidence standards. That editorial discipline — more than any technical decision — is what defines the quality of the project.
